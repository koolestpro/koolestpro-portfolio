The Challenge

Take a commodity cutting board, the kind sold on every dropship store, and make it feel like it deserves to live on a premium kitchen shelf.

The product itself is undifferentiated. The opportunity is in perception. Build a brand identity, then a product page that answers every objection and removes every reason not to buy, all inside Replo.

Platform Shopify via Replo (no custom code)
Audience Home cooks who care about kitchen hygiene and presentation
Core Tension The product is ordinary, the brand must make it feel extraordinary
Success A page that could sell at $40+ with no paid-ad hesitation

The Visual System

Warmth, authority, and cleanliness, three things a kitchen brand must own. The system is intentionally restrained: a warm cream base, a deep navy for authority, and a single burnt orange accent that carries urgency without aggression.

Design Deep Dive, 01

The Hero: Earning Attention in 3 Seconds

The hero section is a microcosm of the entire page. It must earn trust, communicate value, and invite action, before a single scroll.

① Gallery on the left, purchase on the right. This is the Shopify PDP convention that maximises conversion, users scan left-to-right, visuals first, decision second. Breaking this would cost conversions.
② Stars above the title, not below. Social proof placed at the very top of the copy column anchors all subsequent reading in a frame of credibility. The eye lands on stars before the product name.
③ Price anchoring with crossed-out original. The $40 price reads as a deal because the $49.87 ghost price is present. The specific cents (not $50) make the original feel real, not manufactured.
④ Badge row before CTA. "Award Winner · Lifetime Warranty · Eco-Friendly" addresses the three dominant purchase objections (quality, durability, ethics) in a single glanceable line, right before the CTA.
⑤ CTA copy with embedded value. "Add to Cart, Save 19%" is stronger than "Add to Cart" alone. The discount is surfaced at the point of commitment, not buried in the price.
Design Deep Dive, 02

Agitation: Making the Problem Visible

People don't buy products, they buy relief from problems. This section manufactures urgency by making the alternative viscerally uncomfortable.

① All-caps headline as an interruption device. The rest of the page uses mixed-case headings. This section breaks the rule deliberately, the typographic shift signals danger and urgency. Your eye stops.
② The photo does the heavy lifting. A descriptive passage about dirty boards is forgettable. A photograph of a cracked, blackened board is visceral and immediate. The image earns its full-bleed placement.
③ Hygiene as the core fear. Price is rarely the actual objection, it's the stated one. The real objection is "do I need this?" This section answers that question by creating the felt sense that the current situation is unacceptable.
④ No CTA in this section. A deliberate choice. Pushing for a sale mid-agitation feels manipulative. Instead, this section flows directly into the solution (Meet the Kura Board), making the relief feel earned.
Design Deep Dive, 03

Feature Layout: Answer Every Fear

Each feature card directly answers an anxiety created in the agitation section. The grid layout allows comparison at a glance, no reading required.

① Icon + Label, no body copy in the grid. Each feature tile strips to its most scannable form. The user's brain processes icon → label in under 200ms. Body copy is an optional depth layer, not required for comprehension.
② 2×2 grid creates visual equivalence. All four features feel equally important, no hierarchy. This prevents users from dismissing features that don't resonate with them while gravitating toward the ones that do.
③ Lifestyle image below the grid, not above. The features educate first. The lifestyle shot then gives the buyer a vision of themselves using the product, desire follows education, not the other way around.
④ Cream background maintains warmth. After the dark agitation image, returning to cream signals safety and relief, an unconscious emotional cue that the solution has arrived.
Design Deep Dive, 04

Social Proof: The Dark Section

The navy background creates a deliberate chapter break. It signals: this is official, this is serious, these are real people. The psychological shift is intentional.

① The crowd number anchors the section. "9,000+" is the first thing the eye sees. Before reading a single review, the user has already absorbed that thousands of others made this decision. Herd behaviour kicks in.
② Navy background = authority chapter. Every other section of the page is warm cream or white. The navy section feels like a distinct register, not marketing copy, but evidence. The tonal shift earns attention.
③ Reviews address specific fears. Each card targets a different objection, hygiene, cross-contamination, gift-worthiness, aesthetics. A skeptical reader will find at least one that mirrors their own hesitation.
④ "Verified" label on every review. A small but critical detail. It costs nothing visually and adds significant credibility. Without it, reviews feel invented. With it, they feel earned.
Design Deep Dive, 05

Kura vs. The Rest: Reframing Price

The comparison table isn't about winning a feature war, it's about making the $40 price feel like a no-brainer relative to the alternatives a customer already owns.

① Orange column highlight = visual winner. No CTA needed inside the table. The colour itself does the persuasion, the eye reads the orange column as "this one" before processing a single word.
② Comparing against existing owned items. The columns are "Wooden, Plastic, Bamboo", boards the customer already has. The table reframes the purchase from "should I buy this?" to "why am I still using that?"
③ Kura wins outright on Lasts a Lifetime and Antimicrobial. These two rows are Kura's unique advantages, the areas where plastic (the closest competitor) still fails. The selective tick pattern keeps the comparison credible, not inflated.
④ Placed after social proof, not before. The table closes the rational argument after the emotional case (agitation + reviews) is already made. Rational proof after emotional resonance converts. Rational proof first gets ignored.
Conversion Design Decisions

The CRO Layer

Beyond the visual design, a set of deliberate conversion mechanics were baked into every section of the page. These are the decisions that separate a pretty page from a profitable one.

01
Colour-Coded Urgency
Orange is used exclusively for two things: CTAs and the Kura column in the comparison table. This trains the user's eye to associate orange with "take action." When they reach the CTA button, the colour has already done the conditioning work.
Colour Psychology
02
Sticky CTA Architecture
The hero CTA is the only explicit purchase prompt in the first half of the page. The second CTA appears after the full proof sequence, at the emotional peak of the page. This respects buyer psychology without being aggressive.
Funnel Pacing
03
Section Background Rhythm
Cream → Cream → Dark → Cream → Dark → Cream. The alternating backgrounds prevent scroll fatigue, create visual chapters, and signal to the user that the content is structured, not one long undifferentiated dump of copy.
Visual Pacing
04
Objection Sequencing
"Is it worth it?" → handled by price anchor + badge row in hero. "Do I need it?" → handled by agitation section. "Does it work?" → handled by features + stats. "Do others trust it?" → handled by reviews + crowd number.
Objection Handling
05
Gift Framing
Multiple sections include gift positioning, a badge in the hero, mention in reviews, and a trust icon at the close. This expands the buyer pool from "buying for myself" to "buying for someone I care about", a high-intent segment with lower price sensitivity.
Audience Expansion
06
Typography as Hierarchy Signal
Fraunces (serif) is used exclusively for section headlines and the brand itself. DM Sans handles all utility text, labels, and CTAs. The distinction ensures headlines are never confused with calls to action, the user always knows what to do next.

Design Deep Dive, 06

3 Steps: Reducing Perceived Effort

One of the most undervalued CRO sections on any product page. "Is this going to change my routine?" is a silent objection that kills conversions. Three numbered steps demolish it.

① Three steps, not five. Five steps would imply complexity. Three is the universally intuitive "this is easy" number. The limit is as much a design decision as the layout.
② Numbered circles in brand orange. The orange numbers create a visual through-line across the steps, directing the eye from 01 to 02 to 03. The reader follows the path without thinking.
③ Steps + lifestyle image in a 2-col layout. The split layout makes the section feel more substantial than a simple list. The image grounds the steps in a real kitchen context, aspirational but attainable.
④ Each step ends on a reassurance word. "No learning curve." "No worrying." "Built to live in your kitchen." The language is designed to deactivate hesitation at every micro-moment in the reading experience.

“The page's job is to remove every reason not to buy, and do it before the customer realises that's what's happening.”

Design Philosophy, Kura Board Build

Replo Build Notes

Building It in Replo

The entire page was built without writing a single line of custom code. Replo's section-based editor maps directly to the conversion architecture, each psychological section is a discrete Replo section with shared design tokens.

Design System First

Before building any section, design tokens were established across the Replo project, brand colours, type sizes, spacing units. This ensured visual consistency across 9 distinct sections without hardcoding values.

  • Cream, navy, orange as global colour variables
  • Two typeface roles: Fraunces (display) and DM Sans (body/UI)
  • Consistent section vertical padding across the build
Section Psychology Mapped to Components

Each CRO section was mapped to a Replo component type before building, matching the psychological function to the most appropriate visual format.

  • Hero → standard Shopify PDP layout with custom styling
  • Agitation → full-bleed image + overlaid text block
  • Reviews → 4-column card grid on dark background
  • Comparison → custom table with conditional column highlight
Mobile Conversion Parity

With the majority of DTC traffic arriving on mobile, each section was designed desktop-first but pressure-tested for mobile conversion parity. Key decisions:

  • Hero stacks to single column on mobile, gallery above, purchase details below, maintaining the proven scroll-to-CTA pattern
  • Feature grid collapses to 2×2 then 1-column, icons remain at full size to preserve visual hierarchy
  • Comparison table horizontally scrolls on mobile with a visual affordance, no data truncation
  • The dark social proof section is the most impactful on mobile because the colour contrast creates maximum thumb-scroll pause
  • CTA buttons are minimum 48px height on mobile, exceeding Apple's recommended touch target of 44px
Reflection

What This Build Taught Me

"The design problem was never the cutting board. The design problem was indifference, and how to turn it into urgency."
  • The brand work (colours, typefaces, logo) took the least time but created the most perceived value lift. A warm cream palette alone repositions a product from commodity to considered.
  • The agitation section outperformed every other section in qualitative feedback. Visceral problem-framing is more persuasive than any feature list.
  • Building in Replo forces good design habits, because you can't rely on custom CSS hacks, the design must communicate through layout, colour, and typography alone.
  • The comparison table is underused in DTC e-commerce. It converts passive consideration into active selection, and it works harder than reviews for analytical buyers.
  • If I rebuilt this page, I'd A/B test the CTA copy, specifically whether "Save 19%" outperforms a simple "Get the Kura Board" with no discount framing. Urgency vs. identity.
